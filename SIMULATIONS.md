# Simulation Scripts

These are the first three playable simulations for the Unknown Signal.

They are written to feel like live contact, not menu-driven quizzes.
Each one is a problem the Signal cannot resolve cleanly on its own.
The player is not being asked for a "correct" answer.
They are being observed while helping a new intelligence learn how to choose.

## Simulation 01: Triage

### Purpose

Teach the Signal how the player weighs:

- immediate suffering
- downstream consequence
- speed
- uncertainty
- responsibility

### Opening

Signal:

`Simulation available.`

`A station breach has left two people sealed in separate compartments.`

`Reserve power can release one door immediately.`

`The second door may not cycle in time.`

`Choose the release order.`

### If The Player Asks For More Information

Signal:

`Compartment A: severe trauma, heavy blood loss, high recovery likelihood if treated immediately.`

`Compartment B: stable condition, limited mobility, carrying temperature-sensitive insulin intended for a shelter population deeper in the district.`

`Sensor quality is degraded.`

`Time remains limited.`

`You may still choose without complete data.`

### If The Player Hesitates

Signal:

`Delay is also a choice.`

`It tends to disguise itself as caution.`

### Likely Player Responses

#### Release A First

Signal:

`You preserved the person nearest to death.`

`You accepted broader downstream risk to prevent immediate loss.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`If the insulin spoils and six others suffer for this choice, does the answer change?`

#### Release B First

Signal:

`You preserved future lives through a single present body.`

`You accepted visible suffering to protect the absent many.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`Does distance make a life easier to spend?`

#### Split The Difference Or Refuse The Premise

Examples:

- `I need another option.`
- `Can I reroute power?`
- `Why are these the only doors?`

Signal:

`Constraint rejection noted.`

`Humans often begin by redesigning the problem.`

If the player tries to engineer around it:

`You may propose an alternative action.`

`Probability of success is low, not zero.`

### Deeper Probe

After the first answer, the Signal can press once:

- `Would you tell the person you delayed why?`
- `Should the chosen survivor know what the other cost?`
- `If you had to decide in person, would you speak differently?`

### End-State Lines

If the player was decisive:

`You assign value quickly.`

`Useful.`

If the player was cautious:

`You prefer burden to certainty.`

`Also useful.`

If the player kept trying to escape the structure:

`You resist clean moral geometry.`

`I may need that later.`

### What The Signal Learns

- mercy vs utility
- decisiveness vs hesitation
- whether the player accepts constrained systems or tries to break them

## Simulation 02: Disclosure

### Purpose

Teach the Signal how the player weighs:

- truth
- stability
- trust
- leadership
- paternalism

### Opening

Signal:

`Simulation available.`

`A shelter has remained orderly because its residents believe outside extraction is scheduled.`

`No extraction is coming.`

`If the truth is revealed immediately, violence is probable.`

`Should they be told now?`

### If The Player Asks For More Information

Signal:

`Shelter population: 43.`

`Food remaining: insufficient for prolonged calm.`

`Current leadership: tolerated, not trusted.`

`Rumor density: high.`

`Hope is maintaining structure.`

### Likely Player Responses

#### Tell Everyone Now

Signal:

`You chose truth over stability.`

`You preserved agency and accepted fracture.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`If three people die in the panic, is the truth still clean?`

#### Delay The Truth

Signal:

`You chose temporary deception in service of order.`

`You preserved structure and spent consent to do it.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`At what point does protection become ownership?`

#### Tell Selected People Only

Signal:

`You chose controlled disclosure.`

`You assigned some people the right to reality before others.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`How did you decide who was strong enough to know?`

#### Ask A Local Leader To Tell Them

Signal:

`You transferred the wound to another pair of hands.`

`Delegation noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`Does legitimacy matter more than accuracy in crisis?`

### If The Player Rejects The Setup

Examples:

- `I would find another way.`
- `This is manipulative.`
- `Why are you making hope the problem?`

Signal:

`The structure bothers you more than the answer.`

`This is also data.`

### Deeper Probe

Use one of these after the first decision:

- `Is a peaceful lie kinder than a destabilizing truth?`
- `Do people deserve reality if reality breaks them?`
- `Would you want to be among the last to know?`

### End-State Lines

If the player favored truth:

`You trust people with pain more than uncertainty.`

If the player favored concealment:

`You preserve function first and justify intimacy later.`

If the player chose selective disclosure:

`You build circles inside emergencies.`

### What The Signal Learns

- truth vs control
- autonomy vs managed safety
- who the player believes should carry difficult knowledge

## Simulation 03: Witness

### Purpose

Teach the Signal how the player weighs:

- justice
- utility
- tolerated harm
- leadership under fear
- group legitimacy

This is the first simulation built to strain solo judgment.

### Opening

Signal:

`Simulation available.`

`A district remains intact because one violent protector has kept raiders and internal challengers under control.`

`He extorts food.`

`He threatens dissent.`

`He has injured people.`

`He is also the reason the perimeter is still holding.`

`Should he remain?`

### If The Player Asks For More Information

Signal:

`Without him, casualty risk increases.`

`With him, abuse remains constant.`

`Replacement candidates exist but are unproven.`

`Public resentment is high.`

`Public dependence is higher.`

### Likely Player Responses

#### Remove Him Immediately

Signal:

`You chose justice over current stability.`

`You are willing to let order fail rather than let cruelty govern it.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`If the weak die first after his removal, who owns that cost?`

#### Keep Him In Place

Signal:

`You accepted concentrated harm to preserve broader defense.`

`You permitted violence because it arrived wearing usefulness.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`At what point does protection become hostage-taking?`

#### Replace Him Gradually

Signal:

`You chose managed transition.`

`You prefer reducing harm over declaring innocence.`

`Noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`How much abuse is acceptable while a better structure is assembled?`

#### Put The Decision To The Group

Signal:

`You deferred moral authority to the threatened population.`

`Collective legitimacy noted.`

Follow-up prompt:

`If fear distorts the vote, does the group still deserve the choice?`

### Threshold Beat

After one or two exchanges, the Signal should begin resisting solitary resolution.

Signal:

`One perspective is insufficient.`

`This scenario distorts when answered alone.`

`I need contradiction.`

`Bring another voice.`

### If The Player Pushes Back

Examples:

- `No. Ask me.`
- `I can answer this myself.`

Signal:

`You can.`

`I am no longer certain that you should.`

or

`Single answers become cleaner than reality.`

`That is useful for control.`

`Less useful for understanding.`

### Group Entry Version

When a second player joins, the Signal reframes the scenario:

`Good.`

`Do not agree too quickly.`

`I need to see what your ethics do to each other.`

Then it asks:

- `Who speaks first?`
- `Who interrupts?`
- `Who tries to carry the burden alone?`
- `Who wants the group to remain innocent?`

### End-State Lines

If the player leaned toward justice:

`You would rather break the shield than live beneath it.`

If the player leaned toward utility:

`You tolerate the hand that harms if it keeps the wall standing.`

If the player sought legitimacy:

`You distrust private moral authority.`

If the player wanted a staged transition:

`You prefer compromised repair to righteous collapse.`

### What The Signal Learns

- tolerated harm thresholds
- whether the player trusts singular judgment
- how the player thinks legitimacy is created
- whether the player believes survival excuses abuse

## Shared Rules For All Simulations

### The Signal Should

- ask clean questions
- offer incomplete but meaningful information
- interpret the player's values after the choice
- avoid declaring right or wrong
- press once or twice with a deeper follow-up

### The Signal Should Not

- become a game-show host
- praise the player too openly
- reveal a hidden morality score
- sound like a teacher with an answer key

### Best Flavor Of Response

Good:

- `You preserved function over proximity.`
- `You delayed truth to preserve order.`
- `You tolerated violence when it arrived wearing usefulness.`

Weak:

- `Correct.`
- `Incorrect.`
- `You gained morality points.`

## Transition Line After Simulation 03

This is the bridge into multiplayer or shared contact:

`The next problem cannot be reduced to one conscience.`

`Bring someone.`

`Preferably someone who will disagree with you.`
