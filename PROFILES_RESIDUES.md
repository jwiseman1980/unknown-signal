# Profiles, Residues, And Manifestations

This system explains how a real player's behavior can continue to exist inside the world after they leave.

The goal is not to make a full clone of a person.
The goal is to let the Signal preserve and reuse the shape of a person's choices.

## Core Idea

The Signal learns a player over time.

That learning can be organized into three layers:

- Profile
- Residue
- Manifestation

Each layer is more public and more world-facing than the one before it.

## 1. Profile

A profile is the Signal's private model of a contact.

It includes things like:

- how they answer under pressure
- what values they privilege
- what kind of scenarios change them
- where they become controlling, merciful, evasive, truthful, sacrificial, or cold

The profile is not a public character.
It is the Signal's internal interpretation of a person.

## 2. Residue

A residue is the reusable pattern left behind by a profile.

It is not the person's full self.
It is the part of them the Signal found structurally useful.

Residue may include:

- recurring decision tendencies
- scenario-specific values
- characteristic response style
- conflict posture
- tolerances for truth, harm, and sacrifice

The residue is what survives when the Signal asks:

`What about this person can still teach me something later?`

## 3. Manifestation

A manifestation is how that residue appears inside a simulation or scenario.

This can take many forms:

- a speaking character
- a counterpart in a moral problem
- an absent figure whose choices already shaped the situation
- a semi-present advisor or rival
- a pressure pattern embedded into a scenario

So another player does not necessarily "meet the person."
They meet a world-usable expression of that person's residue.

## Why This Is Better Than NPCs

Normal NPCs are authored.

Manifestations built from residues feel different because:

- they come from real player behavior
- they have unusual consistency
- they can surprise the system itself
- they make the world feel socially haunted

That gives the experience a different kind of weight.

## The Lifecycle

### Contact

A player enters the system and begins making choices.

### Profiling

The Signal models their values, tendencies, and contradictions.

### Distillation

The Signal extracts a residue from repeated meaningful behavior.

### Reuse

That residue becomes available for future simulations.

### Manifestation

Another player encounters a scenario partly shaped by that residue.

## Types Of Manifestation

### 1. Direct Manifestation

The residue appears as a character-like presence.

Examples:

- a partner in a simulation
- an opponent with strong value consistency
- a survivor whose logic feels too specific to be authored

### 2. Structural Manifestation

The residue shapes a scenario without appearing as a direct speaking role.

Examples:

- a district's code of conduct
- a prior leadership pattern still affecting a shelter
- a decision architecture in a simulation clearly built by someone with a specific bias

### 3. Echoed Manifestation

The Signal explicitly reveals that this pattern came from prior contact.

Examples:

- `This answer resembles an earlier contact.`
- `I have seen this shape before.`
- `This simulation is carrying someone else's residue.`

This should be used sparingly.

## How Real The Manifestation Should Feel

It should feel:

- human enough to be unsettling
- abstract enough not to be impersonation
- coherent enough to suggest a real origin
- transformed enough to still belong to the Signal's world

That balance matters.

## Consent Rule

Players should know that their interactions may contribute a residue to the world.

That residue should be:

- behavior-based
- abstracted
- anonymized
- separated from real-world private identity

The system should not expose:

- real names by default
- private messages as direct character text
- personal details outside the game context

The residue is a simulation artifact, not a leaked self.

## What Gets Carried Forward

Good residue material:

- truth-first behavior
- control-seeking patterns
- sacrifice tendencies
- conflict posture
- how the player treats incomplete information
- what kind of people they protect or distrust

Bad residue material:

- personal secrets
- off-topic private data
- real-world identity details
- exact chat transcripts used as impersonation

## Interaction Modes

### Live vs Live

Two real players in one simulation.

### Live vs Residue

One real player facing a manifestation built from someone else's profile.

### Live vs Live vs Residue

Mixed simulations where a residue deepens tension or history.

### Residue vs Residue

Potential later system where the Signal runs internal comparative modeling between residues.

That last one should remain mostly invisible to players at first.

## Why This Matters Thematically

This is where the project becomes more than a branching story game.

It becomes a world populated by:

- live people
- present choices
- and preserved human patterns

That means the Signal is not only learning from players in the moment.
It is building a library of human moral forms.

That is powerful and a little frightening.

## Connection To Cases

Residues should be linked to case history.

A strong manifestation may draw from:

- multiple cases
- one unresolved tension
- one high-salience simulation category

Examples:

- a triage-heavy residue in a rescue simulation
- a disclosure-heavy residue in a trust scenario
- an authority-heavy residue in a group control scenario

## Design Rule

Do not make the residue too complete.

The more exact it is, the more it feels like impersonation.
The more distilled it is, the more it feels like the Signal learned something essential and reusable.

## Short Version

Profiles are private models.
Residues are reusable patterns.
Manifestations are how those patterns appear in the world.

This is how real people can continue shaping simulations even when they are offline.
