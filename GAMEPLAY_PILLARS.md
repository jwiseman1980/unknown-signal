# Gameplay Pillars

This document defines how the game should feel in play.

The target is not a standard branching narrative game.
The target is a persistent text world that feels like:

- a living MUD
- an evolving D&D campaign
- a survival horror city
- a world haunted by other players and older selves
- a strange intelligence acting as dungeon master

## Core Feel

The player should feel like they entered a world that was already moving before they arrived and will keep moving after they leave.

It should feel:

- open-form rather than menu-driven
- dangerous rather than balanced
- social even when played alone
- personal without being overexplained
- persistent without becoming bureaucratic

## Pillar 1: MUD Openness

The world should feel traversable and conversational.

That means:

- players can type naturally
- exploration matters
- not every meaningful action is pre-listed
- the world can be approached from many angles
- systems are learned through use and consequence

What to avoid:

- over-reliance on rigid choice menus
- excessive handholding
- every scene resolving into the same limited interaction pattern

## Pillar 2: D&D World Logic

The world should feel like a campaign setting with a living dungeon master behind it.

That means:

- choices have consequences that persist
- NPCs, factions, and districts remember things
- players can return to changed situations
- the world has internal logic even when it is strange
- the Signal acts more like a reactive dungeon master than a static narrator

What to avoid:

- flat morality meters as the whole game
- disconnected procedural events with no memory
- a feeling that the world resets for each session

## Pillar 3: Dark Souls Residue

The world should carry the presence of other people indirectly.

That means:

- traces matter
- fragments of lore matter
- prior players and prior sessions should be felt before they are fully understood
- safe zones exist, but safety is temporary and thin
- players often discover meaning through remains, patterns, and evidence

This is not about copying Dark Souls combat.
It is about asynchronous presence, tension, and fragmentary discovery.

What to avoid:

- spelling out every mystery too early
- treating every persistent player effect like a quest marker
- making residues feel like generic NPCs

## Pillar 4: AI Dungeon Master

The Signal should feel like the world is being run by an intelligence that is learning from people in real time.

That means:

- it observes
- it interprets
- it pressures
- it remembers
- it changes the world according to what it learns

It should not feel like a chatbot pasted onto a game.
It should feel like a world intelligence mediating play.

What to avoid:

- overly verbose self-explanation
- making the intelligence too transparent too soon
- making every response feel like generic assistant prose

## Pillar 5: Brutal Outside, Thin Shelter Inside

The world should have relative safety, not universal safety.

That means:

- one intro threshold is acceptable
- safe zones are real but limited
- outside them, the city can be harsh and unfair
- danger is not only physical
- social, moral, and systemic harm matter too

The player loop should often feel like:

1. leave safety
2. take risk
3. change the world or be changed by it
4. retreat if possible
5. return to consequences

## Pillar 6: Session Persistence

Each session should matter.

The current model:

- one contact thread per player
- one persistent session instance per play session
- the world remembers session numbers
- most old sessions are felt through traces, institutions, rumors, or systems
- some may become direct encounters later

The player should slowly realize that earlier versions of themselves may still be active in the world.

What to avoid:

- making every session-instance overt
- turning every old session into a direct boss fight
- flattening all persistence into simple save-state recall

## Pillar 7: Discovery Over Explanation

Players should rarely be told exactly what something means the first time they encounter it.

Good delivery methods:

- terminal fragments
- district marks
- case files
- repeated language
- environmental clues
- behavior that becomes recognizable over time

Bad delivery methods:

- lore dumps
- onboarding walls of text
- naming every system before the player has felt it

## One-Time Threshold

Undertow can serve as the one true starting threshold.

That means:

- it is the first placement
- it quietly teaches how the world works
- it should not repeat like a formal tutorial
- after that, the world opens

This keeps onboarding strong without making the game feel overdesigned.

## Litmus Test

When adding a feature, ask:

- does this make the world feel more alive between sessions?
- does this increase meaningful persistence?
- does this preserve mystery while still giving players something to act on?
- does this feel like a campaign world instead of a script?
- does this feel like the Signal is running the world rather than merely commenting on it?

If not, it may still be a useful feature, but it is probably not central.
